\subsection{Imaging system}
\label{subsec:imaging_system}
All imaging was performed using a custom-built polarisation-sensitive spectral-domain OCT ophthalmoscope, described in detail previously~\cite{fialovaPolarizationPropertiesSingle2016}. The system was centred at 840~nm with a spectral bandwidth of 100~nm (FWHM), providing an axial resolution of 5.1~\textmu m in air (approximately 3.8~\textmu m in tissue, assuming a refractive index of 1.35). A sensitivity of 96~dB was achieved at an A-scan rate of 80~kHz, with a beam diameter of 0.5~mm at the pupil plane and an incident corneal power of 2.85~mW~\cite{merkleHighresolutionDepthresolvedVascular2021}. This system was employed to acquire volumetric OCT data from mouse retinas before, during, and after administration of an intravascular contrast agent and stimulation using 502~nm light emitting diode (LED) ring flashing at 10Hz.

\subsection{Experimental protocol}
\label{subsec:experimental_protocol}
Three mouse models were used in this study: VLDLR knockout mice (B6;129S7-\emph{Vldlr\textsuperscript{tm1Her}}/J, The Jackson Laboratory, Bar Harbor, USA), C57BL/6 mice \textcolor{red}{(C57BL/6J, The Jackson Laboratory, Bar Harbor, USA)}, and SOD1 transgenic mice \textcolor{red}{(The Jackson Laboratory, Bar Harbor, USA) [n~=~XX per group]}. All imaging sessions were conducted under general anaesthesia using either isoflurane or a ketamine/xylazine combination. Isoflurane was induced at 4\% in oxygen for 4~minutes and subsequently maintained at 2\% in oxygen at a flow rate of 1.5--2~L/min. In cases where isoflurane alone did not provide adequate sedation, an intraperitoneal injection of ketamine/xylazine cocktail (100~mg/kg ketamine, 6~mg/kg xylazine; 10~mL/kg body weight) was administered. To maintain core body temperature of the mice throughout imaging, they were positioned on a heating pad. Mice pupils were dilated with one drop of tropicamide (5~mg/mL, Agepha Pharma s.r.o., Senec, Slovakia) per eye, and artificial tear drops (Oculotect, ALCON Pharma GmbH, Freiburg im Breisgau, Germany) were applied at regular intervals to prevent cornea from drying out.

Intralipid has been shown to be a highly scattering contrast agent that fills the vascular lumen uniformly, thereby compensating for the artefact caused by the diminished backscattering at vessel peripheries caused by RBC orientation~\cite{merkleHighresolutionDepthresolvedVascular2021}. For this study, Intralipid 20\% (3~mL/kg body weight) was administered as a bolus injection via the mice tail vein. Volumetric angiography data were acquired over a 1~mm~$\times$~1~mm field of view centred on the optic nerve head (ONH), with a scan density of 500 A-scans per B-scan and five repeated B-scans at each of 400 slow-axis positions (acquisition time approximately 16~seconds). For this study, pre- and post-injection datasets and pre- and post-stimulation datasets were acquired. Additionally, a dynamic-contrast OCT (DyC-OCT) protocol~\cite{} was employed pre- and post-contrast injection, consisting of 2000 repeated B-scans at the same cross-sectional location (interscan time 8.2~ms) over a 1~mm lateral field of view.~\textcolor{red}{All animal procedures were approved by the ethics committee of the Medical University of Vienna and the Austrian Federal Ministry of Education, Science, and Research (BMBWF/66.009/0272-V/3b/2019)}.

\subsection{Post-processing}
\label{subsec:post_processing}
Cross-polarised OCT intensity data from the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) was used as a reference for inter-frame alignment and B-scan flattening, following previously established methods~\cite{}. Angiographic contrast was computed by applying the complex-difference OCT angiography (OCTA) algorithm~\cite{}. To enhance vessel visibility and suppress background noise, a 3D averaging filter with a kernel size of 50~$\times$~50~$\times$~10 voxels was applied to both the angiogram and DyC-OCT volumes. For the volumetric angiogram data, the kernel dimensions correspond to the axial ($z$), lateral ($x$), and slow-axis ($y$), while for the DyC-OCT data, the same kernel spans the axial ($z$), lateral ($x$), and temporal ($t$) dimensions. For binarisation of the angiographic data, a global threshold was determined for each C-scan based on the maximum angiogram intensity within the smoothed volume. Further details on threshold selection are described in Section~\ref{subsec:diameter_measurement}. The resulting binary masks defined the vessel boundaries used for subsequent diameter analysis. All data processing and analysis were performed in MATLAB (R2019b, MathWorks, Natick, MA, USA).

\subsection{Diameter measurement}
\label{subsec:diameter_measurement}
Vessel diameter was measured using a custom algorithm that identifies the vessel boundaries in the binarised angiogram data. For each vessel segment, the algorithm computes the distance between the inner boundaries of the vessel lumen at multiple points along the vessel length, and averages these measurements to obtain a representative diameter for each segment. To ensure accuracy, the algorithm incorporates a correction factor to account for the point spread function of the OCT system, which can cause an overestimation of vessel diameter. The correction factor was determined empirically by imaging a calibration phantom with known dimensions and applying the same processing pipeline to the phantom data. The final diameter measurements were reported in micrometers, and statistical analysis was performed to compare the diameters across different conditions (pre- and post-contrast injection, pre- and post-stimulation) and between the different mouse models.
