\subsection{Imaging system}
\label{subsec:imaging_system}
All imaging was performed using a custom-built polarisation-sensitive spectral-domain OCT ophthalmoscope, described in detail previously~\cite{fialovaPolarizationPropertiesSingle2016}. The system was centred at 840~nm with a spectral bandwidth of 100~nm (FWHM), providing an axial resolution of 5.1~\textmu m in air (approximately 3.8~\textmu m in tissue, assuming a refractive index of 1.35). A sensitivity of 96~dB was achieved at an A-line acquisition rate of 80~kHz, with a beam diameter of 0.5~mm at the pupil plane and an incident corneal power of 2.85~mW~\cite{merkleHighresolutionDepthresolvedVascular2021}. This system was employed to acquire volumetric OCT data from mouse retinas before, during, and after administration of an intravascular contrast agent and stimulation using 502~nm light emitting diode (LED) ring flashing at 10Hz.

\subsection{Experimental protocol}
\label{subsec:experimental_protocol}
Three mouse models were used in this study: VLDLR knockout mice (B6;129S7-Vldlr\textsuperscript{tm1Her}/J, The Jackson Laboratory, Bar Harbor, USA), C57BL/6 mice, and SOD1 transgenic mice [n~=~XX per group]. All imaging sessions were conducted under general anaesthesia using either isoflurane or a ketamine/xylazine combination. Isoflurane anaesthesia was induced at 4\% in oxygen for 4~minutes and subsequently maintained at 2\% in oxygen at a flow rate of 1.5--2~L/min. In cases where isoflurane alone did not provide adequate sedation, an intraperitoneal injection of ketamine/xylazine (100~mg/kg ketamine, 6~mg/kg xylazine; 10~mL/kg body weight) was administered. Mice were positioned on a heating pad throughout imaging to maintain core body temperature. Pupils were dilated with one drop of tropicamide (5~mg/mL, Agepha Pharma s.r.o., Senec, Slovakia) per eye, and artificial tears (Oculotect, ALCON Pharma GmbH, Freiburg im Breisgau, Germany) were applied at regular intervals to prevent corneal desiccation.

To obtain ground truth vessel diameter measurements, Intralipid 20\% (3~mL/kg body weight) was administered as a bolus injection via the tail vein. Intralipid serves as a highly scattering contrast agent that fills the vascular lumen uniformly, thereby compensating for the diminished backscattering at vessel peripheries caused by RBC orientation effects\cite{merkleHighresolutionDepthresolvedVascular2021}. Volumetric angiography data were acquired over a 1~mm~$\times$~1~mm field of view centred on the optic nerve head, with a scan density of 500 A-scans per B-scan and five repeated B-scans at each of 400 slow-axis positions (acquisition time approximately 16~seconds). One pre-injection dataset and up to three post-injection datasets were collected over the course of 12~minutes following contrast injection for each mouse. Additionally, a dynamic-contrast OCT (DyC-OCT) protocol\cite{} was employed during the initial passage of the contrast agent, consisting of 2000 repeated B-scans at the same cross-sectional location (interscan time 8.2~ms) over a 1~mm lateral range. All animal procedures were approved by the ethics committee of the Medical University of Vienna and the Austrian Federal Ministry of Education, Science, and Research (BMBWF/66.009/0272-V/3b/2019).

\subsection{Post-processing}
\label{subsec:post_processing}
Cross-polarised OCT intensity data from the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) was used as a reference for inter-frame alignment and retinal flattening, following previously established methods\cite{}. Angiographic contrast was computed by applying the complex-difference OCTA algorithm to each set of five repeated B-scans at every scan position\cite{}.

To enhance vessel visibility and suppress background noise, a 3D averaging filter with a kernel size of 50~$\times$~50~$\times$~10 voxels was applied to both the angiogram and DyC-OCT volumes. For the volumetric angiogram data, the kernel dimensions correspond to the axial ($z$), lateral ($x$), and slow-axis ($y$) directions, while for the DyC-OCT data, the same kernel spans the axial ($z$), lateral ($x$), and temporal ($t$) dimensions. For binarisation of the angiographic data, a global threshold was determined for each C-scan based on the maximum angiogram intensity within the smoothed volume. Further details on threshold selection are described in Section~\ref{subsec:diameter_measurement}. The resulting binary masks defined the vessel boundaries used for subsequent diameter analysis. All data processing and analysis were performed in MATLAB (R2024b, MathWorks, Natick, MA, USA).
